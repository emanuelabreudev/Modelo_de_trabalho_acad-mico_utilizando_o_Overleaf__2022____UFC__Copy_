The elicitation of usability requirements remains a recurring challenge in Software Engineering,
particularly due to the difficulty of making usability aspects explicit during early development
phases. The USARP (Usability Requirements with Personas and User Stories) method was
proposed to support the systematic identification, discussion, and specification of usability
requirements through the integrated use of personas, user stories, and usability mechanisms
represented by cards. Although previous empirical studies have reported positive outcomes
related to the method, there is still a lack of a systematic synthesis that evaluates its application
across different contexts and identifies recurring patterns of perception, usage, and adoption.
This work presents an empirical single-arm meta-analysis of the USARP method, grounded
in the synthesis of evidence derived from studies conducted in both academic and industrial
contexts, including primary data and secondary evidence reported in the literature. The adopted methodology combines descriptive statistical analysis, dimensionality reduction through
Principal Component Analysis (PCA), and qualitative analysis, enabling the investigation of
latent dimensions associated with perceived value, operational use of usability mechanisms, and
the level of requirement formalization. The results reveal the presence of real heterogeneity
across contexts, highlighting discrepancies between positive perceptions of the method and its
effective use, as well as differences between academic and industrial environments. Certain
usability mechanisms are consistently adopted, while others present higher cognitive load or
limited practical use. As a contribution, this study provides a critical empirical synthesis of
the USARP method, outlining its strengths, limitations, and implications for its adoption in
real-world software development settings.
